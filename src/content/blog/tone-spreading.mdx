Kinyarwanda is a Bantu language spoken by about 24 million people, primarily in Rwanda. Like many Bantu languages, it uses tone to distinguish meaning. But unlike Mandarin, where tone sits on individual syllables, Kinyarwanda's tone system is dynamic. Tones move.

## The Root Tone

Every Kinyarwanda verb root carries an inherent tone — either High (H) or toneless. The verb *-bóna* ("to see") has a High tone on its first mora. In the infinitive *kubóna*, the H stays put on the root. Nothing moves yet.

## Spreading

When a verb has object prefixes after a **short tense prefix** (like the infinitive *ku-*), the root's High tone spreads **leftward** through the macro-stem — the region containing all object prefixes and the stem.

<CalloutTile color="coral" title="Why This Matters for NLP">

Most NLP tokenizers treat text as a flat sequence of characters. But in Kinyarwanda, the same consonant-vowel sequence can mean different things depending on tone patterns that span the entire macro-stem. A tokenizer that ignores tone spreading produces ambiguous representations.

</CalloutTile>

## Shifting

Not all tense prefixes trigger spreading. **Long tense prefixes** like *-ā-* (today's past) cause the H to **shift rightward** instead — jumping from the root to the 2nd mora of the macro-stem.

<AsideTile label="Meeussen's Rule">

When two High tones end up on consecutive morae, Kinyarwanda enforces a constraint: the second H is lowered. This is **Meeussen's Rule**, shared across many Bantu languages including Shona, Luganda, and Chichewa. It prevents tone crowding and maintains the language's rhythmic structure.

</AsideTile>

## Why This Matters

Processing Kinyarwanda tone in NLP systems raises several hard problems.

**Written text omits tone marking.** Standard orthography doesn't mark tone. Readers infer it from context, morphology, and syntactic position. An NLP system must reconstruct tonal information from these cues alone.

**The same string can mean different things.** Without tone disambiguation, downstream tasks like machine translation produce errors. The spreading and shifting rules above show that the tense prefix alone determines where tone lands — information that's invisible in plain text.

**Training data is scarce.** Large tone-annotated corpora for Kinyarwanda don't exist at scale. This pushes research toward rule-based approaches that encode linguistic knowledge directly — which is exactly what the KINTONE project does.

The exciting direction is building tonal awareness into language models for Bantu languages. Not as post-processing, but as a structural feature of the model. Tone is not decoration on top of text. It's grammar.
